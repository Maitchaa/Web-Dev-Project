Spider Plant (Chlorophytum comosum)

The Spider Plant (Chlorophytum comosum) is a popular, low-maintenance houseplant known for its arching green leaves with white or yellow stripes and small “spiderettes” or baby plants that dangle from the parent plant. It thrives in bright, indirect light but can adapt to lower light levels. Spider plants prefer moderate watering—about once a week—allowing the soil to dry slightly between waterings. They grow well in average indoor temperatures and humidity levels and benefit from moderate air circulation. Spider plants are known for their air-purifying qualities, helping to filter indoor toxins.

Watering

Spider plants prefer to be kept evenly moist but do not like to sit in water. They can tolerate short periods of dryness, but consistent overwatering can lead to root rot.

Frequency

Water sparingly. Let the soil dry completely between waterings to avoid root rot. Typically, every 2-3 weeks in the warmer months and even less in winter.

Technique

Water at the base, not on the leaves, and make sure the excess water drains freely from the pot.

Soil

Well-draining, lightweight potting mix. A standard houseplant mix works fine, but you can add perlite or sand for extra drainage.

Light

Prefers bright, indirect light but can adapt to low light conditions. Too much direct sunlight can scorch the leaves.

Temperature

Best at temperatures between 60-75°F (15-24°C). Can tolerate slightly cooler temperatures, but avoid drafts.

Fertilization

Fertilize every 4-6 weeks during the growing season with a balanced, diluted liquid fertilizer.

Pruning

Cut off brown or damaged leaves and spent flower stalks. You can also trim the "babies" (offshoots) to propagate the plant or maintain its shape.

Humidity

Prefers moderate humidity but will tolerate average indoor levels.

Air Circulation

Moderate air circulation is beneficial for Spider Plants, as it helps prevent moisture buildup around the leaves and roots, reducing the risk of fungal issues. Place the plant in an area with natural airflow, like near an open window (avoiding direct drafts) or a fan set to low.

Pest Management

Spider Plants can be vulnerable to pests such as spider mites, aphids, and scale. Spider mites leave tiny web-like structures on the undersides of leaves, aphids cluster along stems and new growth, and scale appear as small, brown, immobile bumps on leaves.

Repotting

Spider Plants grow quickly and may need repotting every 1-2 years. Repotting is typically required when the plant becomes root-bound, meaning roots have filled the pot or are growing out of drainage holes.
